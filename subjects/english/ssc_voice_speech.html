V.1

The core formula

Every passive transformation, however complex, reduces to this one move.

Active: Subject + Verb + Object

Passive: Object + be (in Active's tense) + V3 + by + Subject
Active: The chef cooks the meal.
Passive: The meal is cooked by the chef.

Four things that change, in order

StepWhat happensExample
1Object of active sentence becomes subject of passivethe meal → The meal …
2Verb splits into "be" (carrying the original tense) + V₃cooks → is cooked
3Subject of active sentence becomes object, introduced by "by"… by the chef
4Pronouns flip to the objective caseI → me, he → him, they → them, we → us

The "be + V3" lock: whatever tense the active verb is in, the passive verb is always a form of "be" + V₃. Identify the active tense first (from the helping verb / time marker), then just plug the matching "be" from the chart in V.2 — never think about the passive tense from scratch.

V.2

Tense-wise transformation chart

The single most-tested table in this topic — memorise the "be" form for each tense.

V₃ = past participle; the object here is "the letter" for consistency
TenseActivePassive
Simple PresentShe writes the letter.The letter is written by her.
Present ContinuousShe is writing the letter.The letter is being written by her.
Present PerfectShe has written the letter.The letter has been written by her.
Present Perfect ContinuousShe has been writing the letter.No standard passive — SSC treats this as "not transformable"
Simple PastShe wrote the letter.The letter was written by her.
Past ContinuousShe was writing the letter.The letter was being written by her.
Past PerfectShe had written the letter.The letter had been written by her.
Past Perfect ContinuousShe had been writing the letter.Not transformable (same as above)
Simple FutureShe will write the letter.The letter will be written by her.
Future ContinuousShe will be writing the letter.Not transformable
Future PerfectShe will have written the letter.The letter will have been written by her.
Future Perfect ContinuousShe will have been writing the letter.Not transformable

The "3 continuous tenses" shortcut: Present Perfect Continuous, Past Perfect Continuous, and Future Perfect Continuous have no clean passive form. If an SSC option tries to passivise one of these, it is almost always the wrong option — eliminate it on sight.

Reading the "be" ladder: is/am/are → was/were → has/have been → had been → will be → will have been. Pick the exact slot matching the active helping verb and you cannot go wrong.

V.3

Modal verbs

can, could, may, might, must, should, would, ought to, need, has/have to

Modal + be + V₃
ActivePassive
You can solve this problem.→This problem can be solved by you.
He must finish the work.→The work must be finished by him.
You should return the books.→The books should be returned by you.
She has to submit the form.→The form has to be submitted by her.
They need not inform him.→He need not be informed (by them).

Never touch the modal. can, could, may, might, must, shall, should, will, would stay exactly as they are — only the verb after the modal changes to "be + V₃". A modal option that changes tense (e.g. "must" → "had to") without instruction is a distractor.

V.4

Imperative sentences

Orders, requests, advice, and prohibitions each get a different passive opener.

Sentence typePassive openerExample
Command / orderLet + object + be + V₃Close the door.
Let the door be closed.
RequestYou are requested to + V₁ …Please help me.
You are requested to help me.
AdviceSubject + should be + V₃Drink milk daily.
Milk should be drunk daily.
Negative commandLet + object + not + be + V₃Do not insult the poor.
Let the poor not be insulted.

Spot the verb, pick the opener: if the sentence starts with a bare verb (Open, Shut, Bring) → "Let". If it starts with "Please" → "You are requested to". No "please" but clearly polite (Kindly, Would you) → also "requested to". A tone word in the question almost always names which opener to use.

V.5

Interrogative sentences

Yes/No questions and Wh-questions passivise differently — word order is the trap here.

TypeActivePassive
Yes/No (Do/Does/Did)Did you post the letter?Was the letter posted by you?
Yes/No (helping verb only)Is she writing a poem?Is a poem being written by her?
What / WhichWhat did you buy?What was bought by you?
Who (as subject → by whom)Who wrote this book?By whom was this book written?
Whom (already object)Whom did you meet?Who was met by you?
WhyWhy did she scold him?Why was he scolded by her?

The Do/Does/Did trick: these auxiliaries carry only tense, no meaning — they simply convert to the matching form of "be" (Do→Is/Are, Does→Is, Did→Was/Were) and the main verb becomes V₃.

Who vs Whom: "Who" as subject of an active question always becomes "By whom" in passive. "Whom" (already the object) becomes plain "Who" in passive, since it now sits as the subject.

V.6

Sentences with two objects

give, tell, teach, offer, show, promise, pay, send, award — each produces two valid passive sentences.

Active: The teacher gave the students a book.
Passive formResult
Indirect object as subject (preferred by SSC)The students were given a book by the teacher.
Direct object as subjectA book was given to the students by the teacher.

Default to the person: when SSC gives four options with only one "correct" passive, it is almost always the version where the indirect object (the person) becomes the subject — "The students were given…" not "A book was given…". Use the second form only if that specific option is asked for.

V.7

Special & frequently-tricky structures

These recur across SSC CGL papers as one-off "different" question types.

Infinitives (to + V₁)
to + V₁ → to be + V₃

I have a lot of work to do.
I have a lot of work to be done.
Causative (get/have something done)
Already passive in structure — do not alter.

He got his car repaired.
(No further passive; this sentence already carries passive sense.)
Quasi-passive (need / require + V-ing)
needs washing = needs to be washed

This shirt needs washing.
This shirt needs to be washed.
Reporting "that"-clause (people/they/one say)
It is said that … / Subject + is said + to-infinitive

People say that he is honest.
It is said that he is honest. / He is said to be honest.
Sentence beginning with "There"
Subject stays after "there"; only main verb turns passive.

There is nobody to help me.
There is nobody by whom I can be helped.
Impersonal / unknown subject (someone, somebody, one)
Drop "by + subject" when the doer is vague or unimportant.

Someone has stolen my bag.
My bag has been stolen.
Common trap Intransitive verbs (happen, occur, arise, appear, die, sleep, rain, exist, seem) have no passive voice — they take no object. If an SSC option passivises "It rained heavily" or "An accident occurred," that option is automatically wrong.
V.8

Active–passive shortcut kit

Run this checklist top to bottom on any transformation MCQ.

#CheckShortcut
1Is the given sentence already passive?Look for "by" + V₃ pattern — if the question says "change to active," reverse the four-step formula.
2Identify the active tense firstUnderline the helping verb / time word (yesterday, tomorrow, since, by 2020) — this fixes the exact "be" form needed.
3Object huntingNo object in the active sentence (intransitive verb) → no passive is possible; eliminate any option that tries.
4Pronoun case checkSubject pronoun (I, he, she, we, they) must flip to object pronoun (me, him, her, us, them) after "by".
5"By + agent" drop testIf the doer is "people," "someone," "they" (unspecified) — the cleanest correct option usually drops "by" entirely.
6Continuous-perfect trapPerfect continuous tenses (has/had/will have been + V-ing) have no passive — reject any option attempting one.
S.1

Core rules of narration

Four moving parts: reporting verb, tense, pronoun, and time/place words.

Direct: He said, "I am tired."
Indirect: He said that he was tired.
ElementWhat to check
Reporting verbPresent tense reporting verb ("says," "will say") → no tense backshift needed in the reported clause.
Reporting verbPast tense reporting verb ("said," "told") → tense of reported clause shifts one step back.
Comma + quotation marksRemoved; replaced by "that" for statements (optional but standard), or left implicit for questions/commands.
PronounsChange according to the speaker/listener relationship — see S.2.
Time & place wordsnow→then, today→that day, here→there, etc. — see S.3.

The one exception that overrides everything: if the reported sentence states a universal truth, a habitual action, or a fact still true at the time of reporting, the tense does not change — even after a past reporting verb. He said, "The sun rises in the east." → He said that the sun rises in the east. (never "rose")

S.2

Pronoun conversion map

The most error-prone step — apply the "1st→subject, 2nd→object, 3rd→unchanged" rule.

Direct speech pronounBecomes (in indirect)
I, we (1st person)Same person as the subject of the reporting verb
You (2nd person)Same person as the object of the reporting verb
He, she, it, they (3rd person)No change — remain as is
She said to me, "I will help you."
She told me that she would help me.

The mirror rule: read the reporting clause first — "Subject said to Object." Then in the reported sentence, every "I" becomes the Subject and every "you" becomes the Object. This single sentence-order check resolves almost every pronoun MCQ.

S.4

Tense backshift chart

One step back on the tense ladder — this is the direct-speech mirror of the V.2 chart.

Direct (inside quotes)Indirect (after past reporting verb)
Simple PresentSimple Past
Present ContinuousPast Continuous
Present PerfectPast Perfect
Simple PastPast Perfect
Past ContinuousPast Perfect Continuous
Past PerfectNo change (already the "oldest" tense)
Simple Future (will/shall)would
Future Continuouswould be + V-ing

The "one rung down the ladder" image: Present → Past → Past Perfect is a single ladder with three rungs. A past reporting verb always pushes the tense down exactly one rung. Past Perfect is the bottom rung, so it never moves further.

S.5

Modal verb shift

Modals shift on a fixed, memorisable list — the most commonly tested single table in this topic.

DirectIndirect
cancould
maymight
willwould
shall (statement)would
shall (offer/suggestion: "Shall I…?")should
must (obligation, one-time)had to
must (rule/strong advice/certainty)no change
could, would, should, might, ought to, used to, need notno change

"Must" is the only fork in the road. If "must" expresses a one-off obligation ("I must finish this today") → had to. If it expresses a rule, strong recommendation, or logical certainty ("You must respect elders" / "He must be tired") → must stays must. SSC tests this fork often — read for obligation vs certainty before choosing.

S.6

Sentence-type variations

Statements, questions, commands, exclamations, and wishes each need a different reporting verb and connector.

1. Assertive (statement)

He said, "I am happy." → He said that he was happy.

2. Interrogative — Yes/No question

Reporting verb "said to" → "asked"; connector "if / whether"; word order reverts to statement order (no auxiliary inversion); question mark removed.
She said to him, "Do you like tea?" → She asked him if/whether he liked tea.

3. Interrogative — Wh-question

"said to" → "asked"; the Wh-word itself becomes the connector (no "that," no "if").
He said to me, "Where do you live?" → He asked me where I lived.

4. Imperative — command / request / advice

ToneReporting verbConnectorExample
Orderordered / commandedto + V₁ (affirmative) / not to + V₁ (negative)"Leave the room!"
He ordered him to leave the room.
Requestrequestedto + V₁"Please help me."
She requested him to help her.
Adviceadvisedto + V₁"You should exercise daily."
He advised him to exercise daily.

5. Exclamatory sentences

"said" → exclaimed with joy/sorrow/regret (matched to the emotion); "that" clause replaces the exclamation; interjections (Alas!, Hurrah!, What a…, How…) are converted into descriptive phrases.
She said, "Alas! I have failed." → She exclaimed with sorrow that she had failed.
He said, "What a beautiful scene it is!" → He exclaimed that it was a very beautiful scene.

6. Optative sentences (wishes / prayers)

"said" → wished / prayed
He said, "May you live long!" → He wished/prayed that she might live long.

7. "Let" sentences

Meaning of "let"Reporting verb / structure
Proposal (Let us…)suggested/proposed that … should
Permission (Let him go)allowed him to go
Assumption (Let AB be a triangle)suppose/assume — usually unchanged
He said, "Let us go for a walk." → He suggested that they should go for a walk.
S.7

Direct–indirect shortcut kit

A five-question checklist to run on any narration MCQ.

#Ask yourselfShortcut
1What is the sentence type inside the quotes?Statement → "that"; Yes/No question → "if/whether"; Wh-question → the Wh-word itself; Command → "to + V1".
2Is the reporting verb in the past tense?Only then apply tense backshift (S.4) and time/place change (S.3). Present/future reporting verb → leave tense, time, and place untouched.
3Whose "I" and whose "you"?Use the mirror rule from S.2 before touching anything else — this alone resolves most wrong-option traps.
4Any emotion word (Alas, Hurrah, What, How)?Switch reporting verb to "exclaimed with joy/sorrow/wonder" and drop the interjection.
5Any modal inside the quotes?Apply the S.5 table; remember "must" is the only verb needing a judgement call.
E.1

How SSC CGL actually frames these questions

Recurring question formats seen across previous Tier-I and Tier-II papers.

Pattern A — Direct conversionSelect the sentence with correct passive/indirect form of the given sentence. (4 options, one correct — the other three each break one rule from V.7/S.7 above.)
Pattern B — Error spotting within voice/narrationA sentence is divided into parts; one part contains a voice/narration error (wrong "be" form, missed pronoun shift, wrong tense backshift). Select that part.
Pattern C — Fill in the correct passive/indirect formA sentence with a blank; four word-groups are given as options to complete it correctly.
Pattern D — "No improvement required"One of the four options is often "No improvement," used when the original sentence is already a correct transformation — don't assume an error must exist.
Pattern E — Mixed sentence typesQuestions increasingly combine a modal + interrogative ("Can you tell me where he lives?") or an imperative + negative ("Don't waste your time") — solve these by applying the checklists (V.8/S.7) rule by rule rather than pattern-matching to a memorised sentence.
Where most marks are lost Confusing "whom" (already object) with "who" (subject) in interrogative passive; forgetting the universal-truth exception in narration; applying tense backshift even when the reporting verb is in present tense; and choosing "a book was given to the students" over the SSC-preferred "the students were given a book."
E.2

The 60-second drill

Run through this sequence on every single question before marking an answer.

For Active–Passive
  1. Find the tense from the helping verb.
  2. Check the verb is transitive (has an object).
  3. Match the "be" form from the V.2 ladder.
  4. Flip subject↔object and the pronoun case.
  5. Check for a two-object or infinitive/quasi-passive trap.
For Direct–Indirect
  1. Classify the sentence type inside the quotes.
  2. Check if the reporting verb is past — decide if backshift applies.
  3. Apply the pronoun mirror rule.
  4. Convert time/place words only if step 2 says yes.
  5. Check for the universal-truth exception and the "must" fork.
