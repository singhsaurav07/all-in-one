Reasoning is officially called "General Intelligence & Reasoning" in SSC CGL. It's one of the highest scoring, most time-efficient sections in the exam because it doesn't test static knowledge — it tests pattern recognition and speed, which improves purely with practice.

This guide covers both stages: Tier 1 (qualifying, 25 Qs) and Tier 2 Paper 1, Section 1, Module 2 (counts toward merit, 30 Qs, harder).

🔹 Tier 1 — Full Topic List

25 questions, 2 marks each, 50 total marks. No sectional time limit (60 min shared across all 4 sections). Mix of verbal and non-verbal reasoning, generally moderate difficulty designed to test quick thinking.

Verbal Reasoning

  • Analogy — Semantic, Number, and Figural analogy (word/number/shape relationships)
  • Classification — Semantic and Symbolic/Number classification (odd one out)
  • Series — Number series, Alphabet series, Semantic series
  • Coding-Decoding — letter/number/symbol coding patterns
  • Blood Relations — family tree and relationship puzzles
  • Direction & Distance — direction sense tests
  • Syllogistic Reasoning — statement-conclusion, Venn diagram based logic
  • Statement & Conclusion / Assumption
  • Word Building — arranging letters, forming words
  • Arranging words in meaningful/dictionary order
  • Date & City Matching
  • Address Matching
  • Indexing

Non-Verbal / Visual Reasoning

  • Figural Classification and Figural Analogy
  • Non-verbal series (pattern completion in figures)
  • Space Orientation / Space Visualisation
  • Spatial Orientation
  • Symbolic operations / Mathematical operations
  • Matrix-based questions
  • Venn Diagrams

Analytical / Judgement-based

  • Problem-Solving
  • Critical Thinking
  • Judgement & Decision Making
  • Observation
  • Discrimination
  • Analysis
  • Relationship Concepts
  • Emotional Intelligence
  • Social Intelligence

🔸 Tier 2 — Full Topic List

Officially: Module II of Section I, Paper I. 30 questions, 3 marks each, 90 total marks, with sectional timing. The topic list is broadly the same as Tier 1, but expect harder puzzles, tighter time pressure, and more emphasis on seating arrangement, complex syllogism, and multi-step logic.

Core topics (verbal + non-verbal, both stages)

  • Analogy — semantic, number, figural (higher-order combinations at Tier 2)
  • Classification — semantic and symbolic
  • Series — number, alphabet, mixed series with 2-3 step logic
  • Coding-Decoding — including coding by letter-shifting, matrix coding
  • Blood Relations — often combined with coded relations
  • Direction & Distance
  • Syllogism — multi-statement, multiple conclusions
  • Venn Diagrams
  • Statement-Conclusion / Statement-Argument / Statement-Assumption
  • Non-verbal reasoning — figure series, mirror/water images, paper folding, embedded figures
  • Space visualisation / orientation

Topics with higher emphasis at Tier 2

  • Seating Arrangement (linear, circular, complex multi-condition puzzles)
  • Puzzles — floor-based, box-based, ranking puzzles
  • Ranking & Order — position-based logic
  • Word Formation
  • Missing Numbers / Matrix-based number puzzles
  • Logical Reasoning (broader critical-reasoning style questions)
Key point: SSC doesn't publish two separate topic lists — Tier 2 draws from the same pool as Tier 1, but examiners raise the difficulty by stacking conditions (e.g. a seating arrangement question with 3 extra constraints) rather than introducing new topic names. Treat your Tier 1 prep as the foundation, then layer in speed and multi-condition puzzle practice for Tier 2.

⚖️ Tier 1 vs Tier 2 — What Actually Changes

🔴 Tier 1

  • 25 Qs, 2 marks each
  • Qualifying only — not counted in final merit
  • Moderate difficulty, tests basic speed
  • No sectional timer (shared 60 min)
  • -0.5 negative marking
  • Simpler puzzles, single-condition logic

🟣 Tier 2

  • 30 Qs, 3 marks each
  • Counts directly toward final merit/rank
  • Higher difficulty, multi-step logic
  • Sectional timing applies
  • -1 negative marking
  • Complex seating/puzzle arrangements with multiple constraints

Same topic pool, different depth

SSC does not add "new" reasoning topics at Tier 2 — it deepens the same ones. A Tier 1 seating arrangement question might involve 5 people around a table; a Tier 2 version might involve 8 people, 2 extra conditions, and require you to eliminate possibilities in multiple passes. Master the Tier 1 list first — it's the complete foundation for both stages.

🚀 Preparation Strategy

1Master non-verbal first

It's the highest-weightage single topic and the one most people under-practice. Drill figure series, mirror images, and paper cutting/folding until they're instant.

2Time yourself from day one

Reasoning rewards speed more than depth. Practice every topic against a stopwatch, not just for accuracy.

3Build a "shortcut bank"

Coding-decoding, syllogism, and number series all have reusable shortcut techniques — learn the technique, not just the answer.

4Puzzles need dedicated practice

Seating arrangement and box/floor puzzles are the biggest jump in difficulty from Tier 1 to Tier 2 — practice multi-condition puzzles specifically for Tier 2.

5Don't guess blindly

With -0.5 (Tier 1) and -1 (Tier 2) negative marking, only attempt when you're reasonably confident — reasoning is one of the few sections where wrong answers are truly avoidable with practice.

6Use PYQs over random practice sets

SSC's question style is fairly consistent year to year — solving actual previous year papers teaches the exact phrasing and trap patterns better than generic practice books.

7Revise low-frequency topics last

Spend minimal time on rare topics like address/date matching or indexing — a quick concept pass is enough, don't over-invest here.

8Mock tests under real conditions

Since Tier 1 has no sectional timer but Tier 2 does, simulate both conditions separately as you get closer to each exam.
