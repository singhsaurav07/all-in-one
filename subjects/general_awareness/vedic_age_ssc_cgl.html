1

Early Vedic (Rigvedic) Polity & Society

c. 1500–1000 BCE · Sapta Sindhu region
Very High PYQ
▾

Timeline & Geography

The Early Vedic (Rigvedic) Age is dated roughly 1500–1000 BCE. The Indo-Aryans first settled in the Sapta Sindhu ("land of seven rivers") — the region covering Punjab and parts of Afghanistan, watered by the Indus and its tributaries. The Rig Veda mentions the Sindhu (Indus) most frequently, and the Saraswati is called "Naditama" (best of rivers).

Political Organisation

  • Jana — the tribe; the largest political unit, headed by the Rajan (chief/king). Kingship was largely non-hereditary at this stage and depended on tribal consent.
  • Vish → Grama → Kula — descending administrative/social units. The Kula (family) was the smallest unit, headed by the Kulapa.
  • Key officials assisting the Rajan: Purohita (chief priest/advisor), Senani (army commander), Gramani (village head, later a military leader too).
  • No regular taxation system or standing army; the king depended on voluntary gifts (Bali) from the tribe.

Tribal Assemblies

AssemblyFunction
SabhaCouncil of elders; select/wealthy members; also acted in a judicial capacity. Women (Sabhavati) could attend.
SamitiGeneral tribal assembly of the people; discussed policy matters; could even elect/re-elect the Rajan.
VidathaOldest assembly — a folk gathering for religious, social, economic & military purposes. Notable for the participation of women.
GanaA general term for tribal republican-type assembly/group.
PYQ Trap: Don't confuse Sabha (elite council) with Samiti (general assembly) — Sabha is often called the "assembly of elders/nobles" while Samiti is the "national assembly of the people."

Society

  • Society was tribal and pastoral, relatively egalitarian — occupation was not yet hereditary and the rigid Varna system had not fully crystallised (it emerges only towards the very end of this period, referenced in the Purusha Sukta, a later addition to the 10th Mandala).
  • Patriarchal, but women enjoyed a relatively respectable position — they attended assemblies (Sabha, Vidatha), and some, like Ghosha, Lopamudra, Apala, Vishwavara, composed Vedic hymns.
  • Marriage was generally monogamous (though polygamy existed among chiefs); child marriage and Sati were absent. Practices like Niyoga are attested.
  • Family was patriarchal (Kula), joint-family system prevailed.

The Battle of Ten Kings (Dasarajna Yuddha)

Fought on the banks of the river Parushni (modern Ravi) between king Sudas of the Bharata clan (Tritsu) and a confederacy of ten tribes (including Purus, Yadus, Turvashas, Anus, Druhyus). Sudas emerged victorious with the help of sage Vasishtha — this event is central to Mandala VII of the Rig Veda.

High-value fact: The smallest unit of Early Vedic polity was the Kula (headed by Kulapa); the largest was the Jana (headed by Rajan). [Asked in SSC CGL Tier 1, 2023]

Later Vedic Period Changes

c. 1000–600 BCE · Ganga–Yamuna Doab
Very High PYQ
▾

Shift in Geography & Technology

The centre of Aryan activity shifted east from Sapta Sindhu to the Ganga–Yamuna Doab (Kuru-Panchala region). Use of iron (called "Krishna Ayas" or "Shyama Ayas") enabled forest clearance and settled agriculture, which became the dominant occupation, replacing pastoralism.

Political Changes

  • Small tribal Janas consolidated into larger kingdoms: Kuru, Panchala, Kosala, Kashi, Videha, Magadha etc.
  • Kingship became more powerful and hereditary; the king performed elaborate sacrifices to legitimise and glorify his authority:
    • Rajasuya — consecration ceremony conferring supreme power.
    • Ashvamedha — horse sacrifice asserting unquestioned control over territory.
    • Vajapeya — chariot race sacrifice, the king's chariot made to win.
  • Sabha and Samiti continued but lost much of their power and became dominated by nobles and Brahmanas; the Vidatha disappears entirely.
  • The Bali (earlier voluntary gift) became a compulsory tax/tribute; other levies like Bhaga (share of produce) and Shulka appear.
  • Administration grew more complex — texts mention 12 Ratnins (jewels/high officials) around the king, including the Purohita, Senani, Gramani, Suta (charioteer), Sangrahitri (treasurer), Bhagadugha (tax collector), Akshavapa (accountant/dice-keeper), Govikartana, Palagala, Kshattri, Takshan, Purapa.

Social Changes

  • The Varna system became rigid, hereditary, and occupation-based, with the Brahmana and Kshatriya asserting dominance over the Vaishya and Shudra.
  • Position of women declined — they were excluded from assemblies, and the birth of a daughter began to be viewed unfavourably in some texts (Aitareya Brahmana).
  • The Gotra system (exogamous lineage) and the four-stage Ashrama system developed in this period.

Varna System & Ashrama

Social stratification & stages of life
High PYQ
▾

The Four Varnas

The origin of the four-fold Varna system is traced to the Purusha Sukta hymn in the 10th Mandala of the Rig Veda, which describes the varnas as born from different parts of the cosmic being Purusha's body.

VarnaOrigin (Purusha Sukta)Traditional Occupation
BrahmanaMouthPriesthood, teaching, learning
KshatriyaArmsWarfare, administration, ruling
VaishyaThighsTrade, agriculture, cattle-rearing
ShudraFeetService to the other three varnas

In the Early Vedic period, Varna was largely based on occupation and was relatively flexible; by the Later Vedic period it hardened into a birth-based, hereditary system, and untouchability begins to appear at its margins.

The Four Ashramas (Stages of Life)

  • Brahmacharya — student life; celibacy, education under a guru (Gurukul).
  • Grihastha — householder stage; marriage, family, earning livelihood — considered the most important, as it supports the other three stages.
  • Vanaprastha — gradual withdrawal to forest life, detachment while still guiding family.
  • Sanyasa — complete renunciation in pursuit of moksha.
Related concept — Purushartha (four aims of life): Dharma (righteous duty), Artha (wealth), Kama (desire), Moksha (liberation).
PYQ Trap: The Ashrama system is not mentioned in the Rig Veda in its developed four-fold form — it fully develops only in the Later Vedic / Dharmashastra period. Don't attribute it to Early Vedic society.

Economic Life & Land Ownership

Pastoral wealth → agrarian surplus
Medium PYQ
▾

Early Vedic Economy

  • Primarily pastoral — cattle (Gau) was the chief measure of wealth; a wealthy man was called Gomat. Wars were often fought over cattle — the word for war, Gavishthi, literally means "search for cows."
  • Nishka (a gold ornament/coin-like unit) was used as a medium of exchange; the economy was largely a barter economy — there was no regular currency or organised trade.
  • Land was not privately owned; it belonged to the tribe/community, and there was no revenue system based on land.
  • Limited agriculture existed (barley — Yava — was known), but it was secondary to cattle-rearing.

Later Vedic Economy

  • Agriculture became the main occupation with the use of iron ploughshares and knowledge of the fertility cycle; crops included rice (Vrihi), wheat, barley, and pulses.
  • Land ownership begins to shift — the king/state started claiming rights over land, though private/individual ownership was still emerging and not fully established.
  • Crafts became more specialised: metal-working (iron), carpentry, leather work, weaving, pottery (Painted Grey Ware culture is associated archaeologically with this phase).
  • Trade expanded modestly; guild-like organisation and money economy became prominent only later, in the post-Vedic/Second Urbanisation phase (c. 600 BCE onward) — not yet in the Later Vedic period itself.
Memory tip: Early Vedic = "Cow economy" (Gau, Gomat, Gavishthi). Later Vedic = "Plough economy" (iron ploughshare, settled agriculture, land tax).

Religious Beliefs, Rituals & Literature

Nature worship → elaborate ritualism → philosophy
High PYQ
▾

Early Vedic Religion

Religion centred on the worship of personified natural forces, propitiated through hymns and simple domestic sacrifices (Yajnas) — there was no idol worship and no temples at this stage.

DeityRepresentsPYQ Note
IndraWar god, rain, thunder ("Purandara" — breaker of forts)Most hymns dedicated to him (~250)
AgniFire god; messenger between men and gods2nd most hymns (~200)
VarunaGuardian of cosmic/moral order (Rta)Upholder of natural law
SomaPlant/drink deity used in ritualsEntire Mandala IX dedicated to Soma
Surya, Ushas, Prithvi, VayuSun, Dawn, Earth, WindNature deities

Later Vedic Religion

  • Worship became more ritualistic and complex; elaborate sacrifices (Rajasuya, Ashvamedha, Vajapeya) performed by kings with Brahmana priests playing a dominant, indispensable role.
  • Prajapati (creator god) rose to prominence; Indra and Agni declined in relative importance.
  • Growing discontent with ritualism gave rise to philosophical speculation — questioning the nature of the soul (Atman) and the universe (Brahman) — recorded in the Upanishads, which mark the beginning of Vedanta philosophy and set the stage for the later Shramana movements (Buddhism, Jainism).
High-value fact: The "Ayurveda" (science of medicine/longevity) is traditionally associated with the Atharvaveda, which contains hymns related to healing, charms and spells. Dhanvantari is revered as the god of Ayurvedic medicine.

Vedas, Upanishads & Vedangas

The literary corpus — most PYQ-dense topic
Highest PYQ
▾

The Four Vedas (Samhitas)

VedaNatureKey Facts
Rig VedaHymns (Suktas) in praise of godsOldest Veda; oldest literary work of the Indo-Aryans; 10 Mandalas, 1,028 hymns; Mandalas II–VII are the oldest ("Family Books"); Mandala X has the Purusha Sukta.
Sama VedaVerses set to musical chants, mostly borrowed from Rig Veda"Book of Chants" — regarded as the origin of Indian classical music (Sangeeta).
Yajur VedaSacrificial formulae (Mantras + prose) explaining ritual procedureOnly Veda partly in prose; two recensions — Shukla (White) and Krishna (Black) Yajurveda.
Atharva VedaSpells, charms, magic, folk medicineLatest of the four; reflects non-Aryan/popular folk traditions; source of Ayurveda.

Four Layers within Each Veda

  • Samhita — the core collection of hymns/mantras.
  • Brahmana — prose texts explaining ritual and sacrificial procedure (e.g., Aitareya Brahmana, Shatapatha Brahmana).
  • Aranyaka — "forest texts," transitional, philosophical-ritual content meant for hermits.
  • Upanishad — purely philosophical texts; also called Vedanta ("end of the Veda").

Upanishads

There are traditionally 108 Upanishads (around 13 are considered the principal/older ones, e.g., Chandogya, Brihadaranyaka, Katha, Mundaka, Mandukya, Isha, Kena, Taittiriya, Aitareya, Prashna, Svetasvatara). They discuss Atman (self), Brahman (ultimate reality), Karma, and Moksha.

High-value fact: The national motto "Satyameva Jayate" is taken from the Mundaka Upanishad. [Frequently asked — SSC CPO/CGL]

The Six Vedangas ("limbs of the Veda")

VedangaDeals with
ShikshaPhonetics, pronunciation
KalpaRituals, ceremonies (Kalpasutras)
VyakaranaGrammar — Panini's Ashtadhyayi is the classic text here
NiruktaEtymology of words (Yaska's Nirukta)
ChhandasMetre/prosody
JyotishaAstronomy/astrology, for fixing ritual timing
Memory tip (Vedangas): "Shuddh Kapde Vale Nete Chand Jaldi" → Shiksha, Kalpa, Vyakarana, Nirukta, Chhandas, Jyotisha.

The Four Upavedas

  • Ayurveda (medicine) — linked to Atharvaveda
  • Dhanurveda (archery/warfare) — linked to Yajurveda
  • Gandharvaveda (music, dance) — linked to Samaveda
  • Shilpaveda/Sthapatyaveda (architecture) — linked to Rigveda/Atharvaveda

Tier 2 — Previous Year Questions

Tier 2 General Awareness tends to go a level deeper than Tier 1 — expect more analytical, comparison-based, and "which of the following is correctly matched" style questions on the Vedic Age.

Tier 2Polity
Q. Which of the following Vedic assemblies played the most direct role in electing/re-electing the Rajan?
  • Vidatha
  • Samiti
  • Sabha
  • Gana
The Samiti was the general tribal assembly with a political character — it discussed policy and could confirm or select the Rajan, distinguishing it from the more elite Sabha.
Tier 2Later Vedic
Q. Consider the following sacrifices performed by Later Vedic kings — match them with their purpose: (1) Rajasuya (2) Ashvamedha (3) Vajapeya — with (a) horse sacrifice for territorial supremacy (b) chariot race sacrifice (c) royal consecration ceremony.
Answer: 1-c (Rajasuya = consecration), 2-a (Ashvamedha = horse sacrifice), 3-b (Vajapeya = chariot race). Tier 2 frequently tests these as matching/matrix-type questions.
Tier 2Economy
Q. Which of the following best describes the change in land ownership from the Early to the Later Vedic period?
  • Private ownership existed throughout the Vedic Age
  • Land was communal/tribal in the Early Vedic period; the king began asserting rights over land in the Later Vedic period as agriculture became central
  • Land ownership was never a concern in Vedic society
  • The Shudras owned all agricultural land
Tier 2 often frames economic-change questions as analytical statements rather than one-word answers — always link the shift to the rise of iron tools and settled agriculture.
Tier 2Literature
Q. Arrange the four layers of Vedic literature in their correct chronological/structural order:
Answer: Samhita → Brahmana → Aranyaka → Upanishad. This ordering (core hymns → ritual explanation → forest philosophy → pure philosophy) is a favourite Tier 2 sequencing question.
Tier 2Society
Q. Which of the following statements about the position of women in the Vedic Age is correct?
  • Women's status steadily improved from Early to Later Vedic times
  • Women had a relatively better position in the Early Vedic period (assembly participation, composing hymns) but their status declined in the Later Vedic period
  • Women held no rights in either period
  • Sati was a common Vedic-age practice
A classic "direction of change" question — Tier 2 likes to test the trend (decline), not just isolated facts.
Tier 2Religion
Q. The concept of "Rta" in Vedic religion, associated primarily with the god Varuna, refers to:
  • A form of sacrifice
  • The cosmic and moral order governing the universe
  • A type of land tax
  • A tribal assembly
Tier 2 GA sometimes tests conceptual/abstract Vedic terms like Rta, Rna, and Yajna beyond simple factual recall.
Tier 2Vedangas
Q. Which Vedanga deals with phonetics and correct pronunciation of Vedic hymns, and which deals with etymology?
Answer: Shiksha = phonetics; Nirukta = etymology (Yaska's Nirukta is the key text). Tier 2 likes to pair up two Vedangas in one question to test depth.
Tier 2Administration
Q. The term "Ratnin" in the Later Vedic period referred to:
  • A land tax
  • A religious ceremony
  • High officials/dignitaries associated with the royal household and administration
  • A type of coin
The 12 Ratnins (including Purohita, Senani, Gramani, Suta, Sangrahitri) reflect the growing administrative complexity of Later Vedic kingdoms — a good indicator of state formation, often tested in Tier 2.
Tier 2Comparative
Q. Which of the following is NOT a correct feature of the Early Vedic period as compared to the Later Vedic period?
  • Cattle were the chief form of wealth
  • The Varna system was relatively fluid
  • Iron tools were extensively used for agriculture
  • Kingship was largely non-hereditary
Iron use is a Later Vedic feature, not Early Vedic — a classic "identify the incorrect statement" format common in Tier 2.

Predicted Questions for Upcoming Exams

Based on a review of question patterns across the last decade of SSC CGL Tier 1 & Tier 2 papers, these areas of the Vedic Age are statistically under-tested relative to their syllabus weight — meaning they carry a higher chance of appearing in upcoming exams. Study these carefully.

PredictedGeography
Q. Match the Rigvedic river names with their modern names: Vitasta, Askini, Vipasa, Shutudri.
Answer: Vitasta = Jhelum, Askini = Chenab, Vipasa = Beas, Shutudri = Sutlej. High-probability matching question — memorise all 7 river-name pairs (including Sindhu=Indus, Parushni=Ravi).
PredictedSociety
Q. Which Vedic-age women are known to have composed hymns of the Rig Veda?
Answer: Ghosha, Lopamudra, Apala, Vishwavara, Romasha, among others — collectively referred to as "Brahmavadinis." This fact hasn't appeared often recently and is due for testing.
PredictedLiterature
Q. Name the two recensions of the Yajurveda and their key distinguishing feature.
Answer: Shukla (White) Yajurveda — mantras only, in verse; Krishna (Black) Yajurveda — mantras mixed with prose commentary. Frequently confused, good elimination-question material.
PredictedUpanishads
Q. Which Upanishad is the source of the famous phrase "Ekam Sat Vipra Bahudha Vadanti" (Truth is one, sages call it by many names)?
Note: this specific phrase actually originates in the Rig Veda (Mandala I) rather than an Upanishad — a good "trap" question testing whether you can distinguish Rigvedic philosophical lines from Upanishadic ones.
PredictedAdministration
Q. In the Later Vedic period, which official was responsible for tax/revenue collection, and which for accounts?
Answer: Bhagadugha = tax/revenue collector; Akshavapa = accountant (also associated with keeping dice for gambling rituals). Detailed Ratnin roles are a likely Tier 2 area.
PredictedComparative
Q. Which of the following pairs is INCORRECTLY matched? (a) Sabha–assembly of elders (b) Samiti–general tribal assembly (c) Vidatha–military assembly only (d) Gana–tribal republican group
Answer: (c) — Vidatha was not purely military; it was a multi-purpose (religious, social, economic, military) folk assembly, notable for women's participation. Assembly-function mismatches are a reliably tested trap format.
PredictedUpavedas
Q. Match each Upaveda with its parent Veda: Ayurveda, Dhanurveda, Gandharvaveda, Shilpaveda.
Answer: Ayurveda–Atharvaveda, Dhanurveda–Yajurveda, Gandharvaveda–Samaveda, Shilpaveda–Rigveda/Atharvaveda. Upavedas are a lesser-tested but syllabus-listed sub-topic — solid predicted-question territory.
PredictedEconomy
Q. What was the significance of the term "Nishka" in Vedic economic life?
Answer: A gold ornament/unit used as a medium of value/exchange in the largely barter-based Early Vedic economy — a precursor concept to currency, worth remembering alongside "Gomat" (cattle-wealthy) and "Gavishthi" (cattle raid/war).
PredictedPolity
Q. Which Later Vedic kingdoms are most frequently referenced in the Shatapatha Brahmana as major political powers?
Answer: Kuru and Panchala (in the Doab), along with Kosala and Videha further east — these territorial kingdom names occasionally appear as standalone identification questions.
