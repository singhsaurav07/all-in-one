🟠 Buddhism at a glance

  • Founder: Siddhartha Gautama (b. 563 BC, Lumbini)
  • Enlightenment: Bodh Gaya, age 35, under Bodhi tree
  • First Sermon: Sarnath — "Dharmachakra Pravartana"
  • Death (Mahaparinirvana): Kushinagar, 483 BC, age 80

🟠 Jainism at a glance

  • 24th & last Tirthankara: Vardhamana Mahavira
  • Born: Kundagrama (near Vaishali), 599 BC
  • Enlightenment (Kevala Jnana): Jrimbhikagrama, age 42
  • Death: Pavapuri, 527 BC, age 72
Bihar Connect: Almost the entire story unfolds in Bihar — Vaishali (Mahavira's birthplace & Buddha's last major stop), Bodh Gaya (Enlightenment), Rajgir (1st Buddhist Council & frequent Buddha residence), Pataliputra (3rd Buddhist Council), Pavapuri (Mahavira's Nirvana). Expect Bihar-location based questions in both Tiers.

Causes for Rise of New Religions

Why Buddhism, Jainism & other sects emerged in 6th century BC

1. Religious Causes MEDIUM

  • Vedic religion had become excessively ritualistic — complex yajnas, sacrifices, mantras
  • Domination of Brahmins as sole intermediaries between people and gods
  • Sacrifices involved animal killing, which was expensive and increasingly opposed
  • Rituals were in Sanskrit, incomprehensible to common people; new faiths used Pali/Prakrit

2. Social Causes MEDIUM

  • Rigid Varna/caste system — Brahmins claimed highest status by birth, not merit
  • Kshatriya resentment against Brahmin supremacy — notably, both Buddha and Mahavira were Kshatriyas
  • New faiths (esp. Buddhism) admitted people of all castes, offering social mobility

3. Economic Causes HIGH

  • Rise of trade & commerce; growth of a wealthy Vaishya (merchant) class denied high status under Varna system
  • Iron technology enabled agricultural surplus, urbanisation, and Mahajanapadas
  • Sacrificial killing of cattle harmed agriculture (cattle = plough animals) — Buddhism/Jainism's ahimsa was economically attractive
  • Both new religions did NOT condemn trade/usury (unlike orthodox Brahmanism) — this won merchant patronage

4. Political Causes LOW-MEDIUM

  • Rise of powerful monarchies (esp. Magadha) needed an ideology beyond tribal Vedic sacrifice
  • New kingdoms/republics (Vajji Sangha) provided a receptive environment for new ideas
🧠 Mnemonic — Causes: "R-S-E-P" → Religious (ritualism) → Social (caste rigidity) → Economic (trade class denied status) → Political (new kingdoms need new ideology)

Mahavira & Jainism

Doctrines, sects (Digambara/Svetambara), literature

Life of Mahavira HIGH YIELD

  • Birth name: Vardhamana; born 599 BC at Kundagrama (near Vaishali, Bihar)
  • Father: Siddhartha (Jnatrika clan chief); Mother: Trishala (sister of Lichchhavi chief Chetaka)
  • Wife: Yashoda; Daughter: Priyadarshana
  • Renounced world at age 30; practised severe austerity for 12 years
  • Attained Kevala Jnana (omniscience) at age 42 under a Sal tree at Jrimbhikagrama
  • Titles: Mahavira (Great Hero), Jina (Conqueror) → hence "Jainism"; also called Nirgrantha (free from bonds)
  • Died at Pavapuri in 527 BC at age 72
Bihar Connect: Kundagrama, Vaishali, Pavapuri — all in Bihar. Pavapuri is a major Jain pilgrimage site today.

Predecessors — 24 Tirthankaras MEDIUM

  • 1st Tirthankara: Rishabhanatha (Adinath) — symbol: Bull
  • 23rd Tirthankara: Parshvanatha — symbol: Serpent; taught 4 vows (Chaturyama)
  • 24th Tirthankara: Mahavira — symbol: Lion; added 5th vow (Brahmacharya)

Core Doctrines HIGH YIELD

  • Triratna (Three Jewels): Samyak Darshana (Right Faith) + Samyak Jnana (Right Knowledge) + Samyak Charitra (Right Conduct)
  • Five Vows (Panch Mahavrata) for monks: Ahimsa (non-violence), Satya (truth), Asteya (non-stealing), Aparigraha (non-possession), Brahmacharya (celibacy)
  • Anuvrata — same 5 vows in a lesser/relaxed form for lay followers
  • Rejected Vedic authority & existence of a creator God; believed in eternal, uncreated universe
  • Emphasised extreme asceticism & self-mortification to shed karma and attain Moksha — this is the key distinguishing feature from Buddhism's Middle Path
  • Doctrine of Syadvada / Anekantavada (theory of manifold predication — reality has many aspects, relative truth)

Split into Sects — Digambara & Svetambara HIGH YIELD

  • Occurred during reign of Chandragupta Maurya, due to a 12-year famine in Magadha
  • Bhadrabahu led a group to Karnataka (Shravanabelagola), retaining strict nudity/austerity → later called Digambara ("sky-clad")
  • Sthulabhadra stayed back in Magadha, allowed monks to wear white robes → became Svetambara ("white-clad")
  • Digambaras: believe women cannot attain moksha without rebirth as men; Svetambaras: allow women monks (sadhvis) to attain moksha

Gautam Buddha & Buddhism

Life, Four Noble Truths, Eightfold Path

EventPlaceSymbol

  • Birth name: Siddhartha; Father: Suddhodana (Shakya chief of Kapilavastu); Mother: Mayadevi (died 7 days after birth; raised by stepmother Gautami)
  • Wife: Yashodhara; Son: Rahula
  • Charioteer who accompanied him: Channa; Horse: Kanthaka
  • Saw the "Four Sights" before renunciation: an old man, a sick man, a dead body, and an ascetic
  • First teacher after enlightenment: gave sermon to 5 ascetics (Panchavargiya) at Sarnath

Four Noble Truths (Arya Satya) HIGH YIELD

  • Dukkha — the world is full of sorrow
  • Samudaya — sorrow has a cause (desire/Trishna)
  • Nirodha — sorrow can be ended (by ending desire)
  • Marga — the path to end desire is the Eightfold Path

Eightfold Path (Ashtangika Marga) HIGH YIELD

#Path element
1Right View (Samma Ditthi)
2Right Resolve/Intention (Samma Sankappa)
3Right Speech (Samma Vaca)
4Right Action (Samma Kammanta)
5Right Livelihood (Samma Ajiva)
6Right Effort (Samma Vayama)
7Right Mindfulness (Samma Sati)
8Right Concentration (Samma Samadhi)

This path is also called the Middle Path (Madhyama Pratipada) — avoiding both extreme luxury and extreme self-mortification (unlike Jainism).

Core Concepts & Symbols MEDIUM

  • Three Jewels (Triratna): Buddha, Dhamma (teaching), Sangha (monastic order)
  • Nirvana — extinguishing of desire, the ultimate goal (not a heaven, but cessation of rebirth cycle)
  • Rejected caste system, Vedic rituals, and existence of soul (Anatta) & permanent God — but did not explicitly deny God's existence like Jainism
  • Preached in Pali, the language of the masses
  • Ashokan symbols: Lotus (birth), Horse (renunciation), Bodhi tree (enlightenment), Wheel (first sermon), Stupa (death) — commonly asked matching set
  • Buddhist Sangha was open to all castes and, later, women (after persuasion by Ananda for Mahaprajapati Gautami)

  • Dhammapada — collection of Buddha's sayings on ethics
  • All originally composed/preserved in Pali

Hinayana vs Mahayana MEDIUM

  • Hinayana ("lesser vehicle") — older, orthodox; does not worship Buddha's image, believes in individual salvation, closer to original teachings
  • Mahayana ("greater vehicle") — later development, worships Buddha as a god/Bodhisattva concept, believes in universal salvation; became prominent after 4th council under Kanishka

Ajivika Sect & Other Heterodox Schools

Often the "trick option" in MCQs

Ajivika Sect MEDIUM — recurring Tier 2 favourite

  • Founder: Makkhali Gosala (also spelt Goshala) — a contemporary & former associate of Mahavira
  • Core doctrine: Niyati Vada (Doctrine of Fatalism/Predestination) — everything is predetermined; effort/free will cannot change destiny
  • Denied karma theory (unlike Buddhism/Jainism) — believed liberation happens automatically after a fixed cycle of transmigrations
  • Enjoyed royal patronage, including from Mauryan rulers (esp. Bindusara, per some traditions) alongside Buddhism/Jainism
  • Practiced complete nudity like Digambara Jain monks

Other Contemporary Schools of Thought (Shramana Movement) LOW-MEDIUM, for elimination-based MCQs

TeacherDoctrine
Purana KassapaAkriyavada — denied any moral consequence of action (Amoralism)
Ajita KesakambaliMaterialism — precursor of Charvaka thought; denied afterlife/soul
Pakudha KacchayanaDoctrine of seven eternal elements (Sassatavada)
Sanjaya BelatthiputtaAjnana Vada — Agnosticism/scepticism, suspension of judgement
Nigantha NataputtaAnother name for Mahavira in Buddhist texts

These five teachers, along with Mahavira, are together referred to in Buddhist texts as the "Six Heterodox Teachers" (Chhalanga) contemporary to Buddha.
