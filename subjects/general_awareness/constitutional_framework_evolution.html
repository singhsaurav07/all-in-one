02 Historical Background — Regulating Act to Independence Act 1947

Eight landmark Acts of the British Parliament, 1773–1947, that progressively built India's administrative and legislative machinery — the direct ancestor of the Indian Constitution.

Mnemonic — sequence of Acts "Ravi Plays Cricket, Grandpa Calls Grandma's Grand India" → Regulating Act 1773 → Pitt's India Act 1784 → Charter Acts (1793/1813/1833/1853) → GoI Act 1858 → Councils Acts (1861/1892/1909) → GoI Act 1919 → GoI Act 1935 → Independence Act 1947

Regulating Act, 1773 HIGH

  • First step by British Parliament to control & regulate East India Company's affairs
  • Governor of Bengal → Governor-General of Bengal (first: Warren Hastings)
  • Governor-General's council of 4 members created
  • Governors of Bombay & Madras subordinated to Governor-General of Bengal
  • Established Supreme Court at Calcutta (1774)
  • Court of Directors made to report on revenue, civil & military affairs to British govt.

Pitt's India Act, 1784 MEDIUM

  • Company's commercial & political functions separated
  • Created Board of Control for political affairs — dual government of Company
  • Company territories in India called "British possessions" for first time

Charter Act, 1793 LOW

  • Renewed Company's trading charter for 20 more years
  • Salaries of Board of Control charged to Indian revenue

Charter Act, 1813 MEDIUM

  • Company's trade monopoly ended (except tea trade & trade with China)
  • Christian missionaries permitted to preach in India
  • Rs. 1 lakh sanctioned annually for education of Indians — first such step

Charter Act, 1833 HIGH

  • Governor-General of Bengal → Governor-General of India (first: Lord William Bentinck)
  • Company became a purely administrative body — trade monopoly ended completely
  • Centralised legislative power in the Governor-General-in-Council
  • First attempt at law codification

Charter Act, 1853 MEDIUM

  • Legislative & executive functions of Governor-General's council separated for the first time
  • Introduced open competition for civil service (Macaulay Committee)
  • Local representation introduced in Indian legislative council

GoI Act, 1858 HIGH

  • Passed after the Revolt of 1857 — ended Company rule, began Crown rule
  • New post: Secretary of State for India (British Cabinet member)
  • Governor-General of India also became the Viceroy (first: Lord Canning)

Indian Councils Act, 1861 MEDIUM

  • Started the process of decentralisation — legislative powers restored to Bombay & Madras
  • Introduced the portfolio system
  • Viceroy empowered to issue ordinances

Indian Councils Act, 1892 LOW

  • Slightly enlarged legislative councils; introduced limited & indirect elections
  • Members could discuss the budget and address questions to the executive

Indian Councils Act, 1909 (Morley-Minto) HIGH

  • Introduced separate electorate for Muslims — Lord Minto called "Father of Communal Electorate"
  • First entry of Indians into the Viceroy's Executive Council (Satyendra Prasad Sinha)

GoI Act, 1919 (Montagu-Chelmsford) HIGH

  • Introduced Dyarchy (dual government) in the provinces — subjects split into "reserved" & "transferred"
  • Bicameral central legislature + direct elections introduced for the first time
  • Separate electorates extended to Sikhs, Indian Christians, Anglo-Indians

GoI Act, 1935 HIGH — most-borrowed source

  • Proposed an All-India Federation of British provinces + princely states (never fully enforced)
  • Abolished dyarchy in provinces; introduced dyarchy at the Centre instead
  • Provincial autonomy granted; bicameralism introduced in 6 provinces
  • Established Federal Court (1937) and the Reserve Bank of India (1935)
  • Single largest source of the Indian Constitution's structural provisions

Indian Independence Act, 1947 HIGH

  • Ended British rule; created two independent dominions — India and Pakistan (15 Aug 1947)
  • Made the Constituent Assembly a sovereign body, empowered to frame the Constitution and also act as the central legislature
  • Office of Viceroy abolished; Governor-General retained as constitutional head of each dominion
  • Abolished the title "Emperor of India" held by the British Crown
Bihar Connect Bihar was carved out as a separate province from the Bengal Presidency in 1912 — a direct product of this colonial administrative evolution, and a commonly asked cross-topic fact in Bihar-level competitive exams.

03 Constituent Assembly — Formation, Composition, Key Committees

The body that drafted the Constitution — its origin, structure, sessions, and the committees that did the actual drafting work.

Origin & Formation HIGH

  • Idea first proposed by M.N. Roy in 1934; formally demanded by the INC in 1935
  • Accepted in principle under the Cabinet Mission Plan, 1946
  • Members indirectly elected by provincial legislative assemblies (not by universal adult franchise)
  • Total strength: 389 seats (296 from British India + 93 from Princely States)
  • After Partition, membership reduced to about 299

Sessions & Landmark Dates HIGH

  • First meeting: 9 December 1946 — Sachchidananda Sinha was temporary/interim chairman
  • Dr. Rajendra Prasad elected permanent President on 11 December 1946
  • Objectives Resolution moved by Jawaharlal Nehru on 13 December 1946 — later formed the basis of the Preamble
  • B.N. Rau served as Constitutional Advisor to the Assembly
  • Total: 11 sessions held over 2 years, 11 months & 18 days

Key Committees & their Chairmen HIGH — repeated every year

CommitteeChairmanKey Role
Drafting CommitteeDr. B.R. AmbedkarPrepared the actual draft of the Constitution — most important committee, formed 29 Aug 1947, 7 members
Union Powers CommitteeJawaharlal NehruPowers to be vested in the Union
Union Constitution CommitteeJawaharlal NehruStructure of the Union government
Provincial Constitution CommitteeSardar Vallabhbhai PatelStructure of provincial governments
Advisory Committee on Fundamental Rights & MinoritiesSardar Vallabhbhai PatelRecommended Fundamental Rights framework
Steering CommitteeDr. Rajendra PrasadSteered the overall procedure of the Assembly
Rules of Procedure CommitteeDr. Rajendra PrasadFramed rules of business for the Assembly
Mnemonic — committee chairs "Ambedkar Drafts, Nehru builds the Union, Patel handles Provinces & rights, Prasad Steers the ship" — Ambedkar=Drafting; Nehru=Union Powers/Constitution; Patel=Provincial/Advisory(Rights); Prasad=Steering/Rules

Working & Adoption MEDIUM

  • Also functioned as India's provisional Parliament from 1947 until the first general elections (1952)
  • Constitution adopted on 26 November 1949
  • 284 members signed the final document on 24 January 1950

Women Members LOW

  • 15 women members in the Constituent Assembly, including Sarojini Naidu, Hansa Mehta, Durgabai Deshmukh, and Rajkumari Amrit Kaur
Bihar Connect Dr. Rajendra Prasad (born in Ziradei, Siwan district, Bihar) was the permanent President of the Constituent Assembly and went on to become India's first President. Sachchidananda Sinha, also from Bihar (Muzaffarpur), was its first temporary/interim chairman. Other prominent Bihar members included Anugrah Narayan Sinha and Braj Kishore Prasad.

04 Sources of the Indian Constitution

The Constitution is often called a "bag of borrowings" — it draws provisions from constitutions across the world while adapting them to Indian conditions.

SourceFeatures borrowed
Government of India Act, 1935Federal scheme, office of Governor, judiciary structure, Public Service Commissions, emergency provisions — largest single source
British ConstitutionParliamentary government, rule of law, single citizenship, Cabinet system, Speaker's office, bicameral legislature
US ConstitutionFundamental Rights, judicial review, independence of judiciary, impeachment, removal of judges, post of Vice-President
Irish ConstitutionDirective Principles of State Policy (DPSP), nomination of Rajya Sabha members, method of Presidential election
Canadian ConstitutionFederation with a strong Centre, residuary powers with the Union, appointment of state Governors by Centre
Australian ConstitutionConcurrent List, freedom of trade & commerce, joint sitting of the two Houses of Parliament
Weimar Constitution (Germany)Suspension of Fundamental Rights during Emergency
French ConstitutionIdeals of Liberty, Equality, Fraternity in the Preamble; Republic
Soviet (Russian) ConstitutionFundamental Duties; ideal of social, economic and political justice in the Preamble
South African ConstitutionProcedure for constitutional amendment
Japanese Constitution"Procedure established by law" (Article 21)
Mnemonic — top 5 exam-favourite sources "Good British Uncles Import Cars" → GoI Act 1935 (structure), Britain (parliamentary system), US (rights & judicial review), Ireland (DPSP), Canada (federal + strong Centre)

05 Preamble — Meaning & Keywords

The introductory statement that summarises the Constitution's guiding philosophy — one of the most repeated topics in SSC Polity.

Core facts HIGH

  • Begins with "WE, THE PEOPLE OF INDIA" — power of the Constitution flows from the people
  • Idea of a Preamble borrowed from the US Constitution
  • Preamble is based on the Objectives Resolution drafted by Nehru (1946)
  • Date mentioned in the Preamble: 26 November 1949 (date of adoption)
  • Amended only once — by the 42nd Amendment Act, 1976

Keywords & meaning HIGH

  • Sovereign — free from external control, can conduct own affairs
  • Socialist — added in 1976; means social & economic equality, not doctrinaire communism
  • Secular — added in 1976; State has no official religion, treats all equally
  • Democratic — government elected by and accountable to the people
  • Republic — Head of State (President) is elected, not hereditary

Justice · Liberty · Equality · Fraternity HIGH

  • Justice — social, economic and political (idea from the Russian Revolution)
  • Liberty — of thought, expression, belief, faith and worship (from the French Revolution)
  • Equality — of status and of opportunity
  • Fraternity — assures the dignity of the individual and the unity & integrity of the nation

Original vs Amended Preamble

Original (1949)After 42nd Amendment (1976)
Sovereign Democratic RepublicSovereign Socialist Secular Democratic Republic
Unity of the NationUnity and Integrity of the Nation
Mnemonic — Preamble keywords in order "Sunny Sells Secret Diamonds Rarely, but Just Loves Everyone Fully" → Sovereign, Socialist, Secular, Democratic, Republic — Justice, Liberty, Equality, Fraternity

Legal Status — Is the Preamble part of the Constitution? HIGH

CaseYearHeld
Berubari Union case1960Preamble is NOT a part of the Constitution
Kesavananda Bharati case1973Preamble IS a part of the Constitution and can be amended, but its basic structure cannot be altered
LIC of India case1995Reaffirmed that Preamble is a part of the Constitution
  • Preamble is not enforceable in a court of law (not justiciable)
  • Called the "identity card of the Constitution" by N.A. Palkhivala
  • Called the "political horoscope of the Constitution" by K.M. Munshi
  • Called the "soul of the Constitution" by Thakur Das Bhargava
Bihar Connect Constitution Day (Samvidhan Divas, 26 November) is observed annually in Bihar's schools and government offices with Preamble reading — a recurring current-affairs-adjacent fact asked in Bihar-level exams.

06 Salient Features: Federal, Parliamentary, Written, Rigid-Flexible

The defining structural characteristics of the Indian Constitution — heavily tested in both direct and "which feature is NOT true" formats.

Lengthiest Written Constitution HIGH

  • Originally: 395 Articles, 22 Parts, 8 Schedules
  • Currently (after amendments): approx. 448 Articles, 25 Parts, 12 Schedules
  • Combines rigidity of a written constitution with practical adaptability

Federal system with Unitary bias HIGH

  • Political scientist K.C. Wheare described it as "quasi-federal"
  • Federal features: dual government (Centre + States), written & supreme Constitution, division of powers, independent judiciary
  • Unitary features: single Constitution, single citizenship, integrated judiciary, all-India services, Centre's power during Emergency

Parliamentary form of Government HIGH

  • Also called the Westminster Model — borrowed from Britain
  • Nominal executive (President/Governor) vs. real executive (Council of Ministers)
  • Council of Ministers is collectively responsible to the legislature
  • Followed at both the Union and State levels

Blend of Rigidity & Flexibility HIGH

  • Amendment procedure under Article 368 has three routes
  • Some provisions: amended by simple majority (like an ordinary law)
  • Some provisions: need special majority of Parliament
  • Some provisions: need special majority + ratification by half the states
Amendment routeRequirementExample
Simple majorityOrdinary law-making processCreation of new states, Citizenship
Special majority2/3 members present & voting + majority of total membership, in each HouseFundamental Rights
Special majority + State ratificationAbove + ratified by at least half the State legislaturesElection of President, distribution of powers between Centre & States

Fundamental Rights, DPSP & Duties HIGH

  • Fundamental Rights (Part III) — justiciable, borrowed from the US
  • DPSP (Part IV) — non-justiciable, borrowed from Ireland; called the "conscience of the Constitution"
  • Fundamental Duties (Part IVA, Article 51A) — added by the 42nd Amendment, 1976 on the recommendation of the Swaran Singh Committee

Other salient features MEDIUM

  • Single citizenship — unlike the USA's dual citizenship
  • Universal adult franchise — voting age originally 21, reduced to 18 by the 61st Amendment, 1988
  • Independent bodies: Election Commission, CAG, UPSC
  • Independent judiciary with power of judicial review
  • Elaborate Emergency provisions (Part XVIII), borrowed from the Weimar Constitution
  • Three-tier government (Panchayati Raj & Municipalities) added by 73rd & 74th Amendments, 1992
Bihar Connect Bihar was among the first states to implement 50% reservation for women in Panchayati Raj Institutions (going beyond the 33% mandated by the 73rd Amendment), under the Bihar Panchayati Raj Act, 2006 — a frequently asked Bihar-specific polity fact.

07 Adoption, Enactment & Commencement

Added as a dedicated tab because the adoption-vs-commencement date pair is one of the single most repeated facts in this entire chapter.

Two dates you must never confuse HIGH — trap question

DateEvent
26 November 1949Constitution adopted by the Constituent Assembly (celebrated as Constitution Day / Samvidhan Divas since 2015)
26 January 1950Constitution came into force — celebrated as Republic Day
  • 26 January was chosen to commemorate the Purna Swaraj declaration made by the INC at its Lahore Session (Dec 1929), observed as Independence Day (old style) on 26 Jan 1930
  • A few provisions (citizenship, elections, provisional Parliament) came into effect immediately on 26 Nov 1949 itself

The final document MEDIUM

  • Time taken to draft the Constitution: 2 years, 11 months, 18 days
  • Signed by 284 members on 24 January 1950
  • Hand-written and calligraphed by Prem Behari Narain Raizada in a flowing italic style
  • Illustrated/decorated by artists of Shantiniketan under Nandalal Bose
  • Original copies are preserved in a helium-filled case in the Parliament Library
Bihar Connect Dr. Rajendra Prasad of Bihar, President of the Constituent Assembly, went on to become independent India's first President on 26 January 1950 — linking this date directly to a Bihar-born constitutional figure.
