Why Magadha, and not Kosala or Avanti?

Geographic & economic edge

  • Fertile Gangetic alluvium around Patna–Gaya gave large agricultural surplus.
  • Iron ore deposits near Rajgir gave Magadha superior weapons and tools before rivals.
  • Nearby forests supplied war elephants — the decisive arm of ancient Indian armies.
  • Control of the Ganga river trade route and timber/mineral wealth funded a standing army.

Political edge

  • Two near-impregnable capitals: Rajagriha (ringed by 5 hills) and later Pataliputra (guarded by the Ganga–Son confluence).
  • Unusually ambitious, long-lived rulers — Bimbisara, Ajatashatru, Mahapadma Nanda — who combined matrimonial alliance, conquest and deceit.
  • Magadha alone kept a permanent, paid standing army rather than relying on levies.

High-yield exam fact

Buddhist texts call Magadha, Kosala, Vatsa and Avanti the "Chaturmahajanapada" — the four most powerful of the 16. By 4th century BCE, Magadha had absorbed all three rivals.

The 16 Mahajanapadas

source: Anguttara Nikaya (Buddhist canon)

"Mahajanapada" = maha (great) + janapada (foothold of a tribe/people). Between the decline of the later-Vedic tribal janapadas and the rise of large territorial states (2nd urbanisation, ~600 BCE), 16 major kingdoms/republics existed across the subcontinent. Two forms of government existed side by side: monarchies (Magadha, Kosala, Vatsa, Avanti, etc.) and Gana-sanghas / republics (Vajji, Malla).

Haryanka Dynasty (c. 544–412 BCE)

Bimbisara → Ajatashatru → Udayin

Bimbisara (c. 544–492 BCE)

  • Founder of the Haryanka dynasty; also known as Seniya/Shrenika; first ruler credited with a regular standing army.
  • Contemporary of both Gautama Buddha and Mahavira; became a lay disciple of the Buddha.
  • Expanded through three strategic marriages: a Kosalan princess (dowry = a Kashi village), a Lichchhavi princess of Vaishali (Chellana), and a Madra (Punjab) princess.
  • Conquered Anga (capital Champa), defeating king Brahmadatta — gave Magadha control of the lower Ganga trade route.
  • Maintained cordial diplomatic ties even with rival Avanti's king Pradyota — sent his royal physician Jivaka to treat him.
  • Capital: Girivraja/Rajagriha, a natural fortress ringed by five hills.
  • Imprisoned and killed by his own son, Ajatashatru.

Ajatashatru (c. 492–460 BCE)

  • Also called Kunika; seized the throne by killing (or imprisoning to death) his father Bimbisara.
  • Fought a prolonged ~16-year war against the Vajji confederacy of Vaishali, eventually winning through his minister Vassakara's divide-and-rule diplomacy that split the Lichchhavi clans.
  • Used two new war-engines credited to his reign: Rathamusala (a mace-fitted chariot) and Mahashilakantaka (a catapult hurling large stones).
  • Also defeated Kosala, annexing it into Magadha.
  • After the Buddha's death (Mahaparinirvana, c. 483 BCE), Ajatashatru sponsored the First Buddhist Council at the Saptaparni cave, Rajagriha — presided over by Mahakassapa.
  • Fortified the village of Pataligrama, at the Ganga–Son confluence, as a frontier fort against a possible Avanti invasion — most SSC-level sources describe this act as him "founding" Pataliputra.

Udayin (c. 460–440 BCE)

  • Son of Ajatashatru.
  • Shifted the capital from Rajagriha to Pataliputra — building up the fort his father had raised at the Ganga–Son confluence into the new seat of government.
  • On the exact wording of "who founded Pataliputra," sources genuinely disagree: some credit Ajatashatru (he built the original fort), others credit Udayin (he made it the capital). If a question asks who fortified Pataligrama, the answer is Ajatashatru; if it asks who shifted/established the capital there, the answer is Udayin.
  • His weak successors could not hold the throne; the Haryanka line ends amid instability, and the people/ministers elevate Shishunaga to power.

Shishunaga Dynasty (c. 412–345 BCE)

  • Founded by Shishunaga, believed to be an amatya (minister) elected/installed to end the instability after the Haryankas.
  • His single biggest achievement: destroyed the power of Avanti (capital Ujjain), ending Magadha's oldest and most persistent rivalry, and annexed it into Magadha.
  • Temporarily moved the capital to Vaishali.
  • Succeeded by his son Kalashoka (Kakavarna), who shifted the capital back to Pataliputra.
  • Kalashoka convened the Second Buddhist Council at Vaishali (c. 383 BCE) — this is where the Sangha split into the Sthaviravadins and Mahasanghikas.
  • Kalashoka was assassinated in a palace revolution, clearing the way for the Nanda dynasty.

Nanda Dynasty (c. 345–322 BCE)

Mahapadma Nanda — founder

  • Founder of the first non-Kshatriya (Shudra-origin, per the Puranas) dynasty to rule Magadha.
  • Titled "Ekarat" (sole sovereign) and "Sarvakshatrantaka" ("destroyer of all Kshatriyas"); also called Ugrasena.
  • Regarded as the first great empire-builder of northern India — extended Magadha's boundaries far beyond its earlier limits, fielding a huge standing army (infantry, cavalry, chariots and elephants, per later Greek accounts).
  • According to Puranic/Buddhist/Jaina tradition there were 9 Nanda kings in total — Mahapadma Nanda plus his eight sons, together called the "Navanandas."

Dhana Nanda — last ruler

  • Last king of the dynasty; identified by Greek writers as Agrammes/Xandrames.
  • Was on the Magadhan throne when Alexander invaded north-west India (326 BCE) — fear of his massive army is cited as a reason Alexander's troops refused to march further east.
  • Extremely unpopular due to oppressive taxation.
  • Overthrown c. 322 BCE by Chandragupta Maurya, guided by Chanakya (Kautilya) — founding the Mauryan Empire and ending this chapter's story.

Common confusions SSC likes to test

  • Pataliputra was not founded by Bimbisara. Ajatashatru fortified the original village (Pataligrama); Udayin later shifted the actual capital there — SSC options frequently swap these two.
  • It's Shishunaga who finished off Avanti, not Ajatashatru or Bimbisara (they only had diplomatic/rival relations with it).
  • Assaka/Ashmaka is the only Mahajanapada south of the Vindhyas — everything else clusters in the north/northwest.
  • 1st Council = Rajagriha (Ajatashatru); 2nd Council = Vaishali (Kalashoka) — years and venues are frequently swapped in distractor options.
  • Mahapadma Nanda is the founder; Dhana Nanda is the last ruler — both get asked, easy to reverse under time pressure.
