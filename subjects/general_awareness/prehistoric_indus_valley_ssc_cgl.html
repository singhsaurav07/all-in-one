Palaeolithic Age (Old Stone Age) High PYQ

  • Period: c. 5,00,000 BCE – 10,000 BCE (Pleistocene / Ice Age).
  • Meaning: "Palaeo" = old, "lithic" = stone (Greek origin).
  • Tools: Made of hard rock quartzite → people called "Quartzite Man". Crude, unpolished chipped-stone tools — hand-axes, cleavers, choppers, scrapers, borers.
  • Economy: Hunting & food-gathering; no agriculture, no pottery, no settled life.
  • Divisions: Lower, Middle & Upper Palaeolithic.
  • First discovery in India: Robert Bruce Foote discovered the first Palaeolithic tool (Pallavaram hand-axe) near Chennai in 1863.
  • Key sites: Bhimbetka (MP, rock shelters & cave paintings), Hunsgi (Karnataka), Attirampakkam (TN), Didwana (Rajasthan).
  • Bihar link: Paleolithic tools reported from the Son River valley in Bihar/Jharkhand belt.
🧠 Mnemonic: Palaeolithic = Pebble/chipped tools, no Pottery, no Production (only hunting-gathering).

Mesolithic Age (Middle Stone Age) High PYQ

  • Period: c. 10,000 BCE – 8,000/6,000 BCE (transitional, earliest Holocene culture) — commonly asked as "12,000 to 10,000 years ago".
  • Hallmark tool: Microliths — tiny stone tools (1–5 cm), shapes like triangles, crescents, trapezes — made of chert, chalcedony, agate.
  • First discovered by: Carlyle, from Vindhyan rock shelters (1867) — hence sometimes called the "Microlithic Age".
  • Transition marker: Beginning of animal domestication — first tamed animal was the dog; evidence of taming from Adamgarh (MP) and Bagor (Rajasthan).
  • Lifestyle: Semi-nomadic; hunting, fishing, food-gathering continues, but settlement near rivers/lakes begins. Belief in life after death — burial of dead with goods.
  • Key sites: Bagor, Tilwara, Sarai Nahar Rai, Bhimbetka, Langhnaj, Birbhanpur, Lekhahia.
📍 Bihar-specific: Mesolithic-Neolithic transitional evidence found at Chirand (Saran district, Bihar) — a site that is very frequently asked in BPSC but occasionally cross-referenced in SSC too.

Neolithic Age (New Stone Age) High PYQ

  • Period: c. 7000 BCE – 1000 BCE (varies by region).
  • Biggest shift: Food-gathering → Food production — start of settled agriculture, domestication of animals (sheep, goat most common), and pottery.
  • Tools: Polished/ground stone tools (celts) — sharper, more efficient than Palaeolithic/Mesolithic tools.
  • Discovered by: Le Mesurier (tools & implements); the concept of "Neolithic-Chalcolithic self-sufficient food economy" was defined by V. Gordon Childe ("Neolithic Revolution").
  • Earliest agricultural site (subcontinent): Mehrgarh (Balochistan, Pakistan) — wheat & barley cultivation from c. 7000 BCE; considered a precursor to the Indus Valley Civilization.
  • Key Indian sites: Burzahom (J&K – pit dwellings, dogs buried with masters), Chirand (Bihar – bone tools from antlers), Koldihwa (UP – cord-impressed pottery, early rice), Piklihal & Utnur (Karnataka), Daojali Hading (Assam).
  • Belan Valley (UP): Unique — shows continuous evidence of all three Stone Age phases (Palaeolithic → Mesolithic → Neolithic).
🧠 Mnemonic: Neolithic = "New" beginnings → Nutrition via farming, No more pure nomadism.

2 Harappan Sites — Mohenjodaro, Harappa, Lothal, Dholavira, Kalibangan & more

The IVC (Bronze Age, c. 3300–1300 BCE; mature phase 2600–1900 BCE) spans Afghanistan → Pakistan → NW India. Tap a site to expand its "unique feature" — this is the single most repeated question type ("Match the following: site → discovery").

Mohenjodaro ▾
Sindh, Pakistan • River Indus • "Mound of the Dead"
Excavated by R.D. Banerjee, 1922. Famous for the Great Bath, Great Granary, bronze "Dancing Girl" statue, steatite "Priest-King" statue, and the largest building at the site (Great Granary).
Harappa ▾
Punjab, Pakistan • River Ravi
Excavated by Daya Ram Sahni, 1921 — first IVC site discovered, hence the civilization's name. Famous for granaries in rows, workmen's quarters, and evidence of coffin burial.
Lothal ▾
Gujarat, India • River Bhogava
Discovered by S.R. Rao. Famous for the artificial dockyard/port — key evidence of maritime trade. Also gave a fire altar, rice husk evidence, and a terracotta ship.
Dholavira ▾
Gujarat (Kutch), India • Between Manhar & Mansar rivulets
Unique three-part division (Citadel, Middle Town, Lower Town), a sophisticated water-harvesting/reservoir system, and a famous 10-letter signboard inscription.
Kalibangan ▾
Rajasthan, India • River Ghaggar (Ganganagar district)
Means "black bangles". Famous for evidence of a ploughed field (earliest attested ploughed agricultural field), fire altars, and pre-Harappan + Harappan layers.
Rakhigarhi ▾
Haryana, India • River Drishadvati (Ghaggar system)
One of the largest IVC sites (larger than Mohenjodaro in area, per recent findings). DNA studies from here informed the "Out of India vs Aryan migration" debate.
Banawali ▾
Haryana, India • Old Saraswati channel
Shows both pre-Harappan (Kalibangan-I type) and mature Harappan phases. A terracotta plough model was found here.
Chanhudaro ▾
Sindh, Pakistan • River Indus
Only major IVC site with no citadel. Known as a centre of craft/bead-making, and lipstick & inkpot-like objects were found here.
Surkotada ▾
Gujarat, India
Provides the earliest evidence of horse bones in an IVC context (debated by historians).
Alamgirpur ▾
Meerut, Uttar Pradesh • River Hindon
Easternmost site of the mature Harappan civilization.
Sutkagen Dor ▾
Balochistan, Pakistan (near Iran border) • Dasht river
Westernmost known site — ~480 km from the Makran coast; likely a trade outpost toward Mesopotamia.
Mehrgarh ▾
Balochistan, Pakistan
Pre-Harappan Neolithic site (c. 7000 BCE) — earliest evidence of farming, cotton cultivation (~7000 yrs ago), and rectangular/square house remains in the region.

3 Town Planning & Drainage System

Grid Pattern Town Planning High PYQ

  • Cities were divided into two parts: a smaller, elevated Citadel (western side — administrative/religious buildings) and a larger, lower Lower Town (residential).
  • Streets were laid out in a grid pattern, intersecting at right angles, dividing the city into rectangular blocks — an extremely advanced feature for the Bronze Age.
  • Streets were wide and straight (not narrow/crooked as in contemporary civilizations).
  • Houses were built of standardized baked (burnt) bricks, in a ratio of 4:2:1 — used uniformly across all sites, suggesting central regulation.
  • Most houses had a courtyard, multiple rooms, a private well, and a bathroom.

The Great Drainage System High PYQ

  • The IVC gave the world's first known covered drainage system — a fact directly asked in exams ("Who first constructed the drainage system?" → Indus Valley people).
  • Every house was connected to a street drain; drains were covered with brick slabs/stone slabs for cleaning access.
  • Soak pits/cesspits were used to collect solid waste before it entered the main drain — showing an early understanding of sanitation engineering.
  • This drainage sophistication is often cited as evidence of a strong, centralized civic authority.

The Great Bath (Mohenjodaro) Medium PYQ

  • Located in the citadel; built of finely fitted bricks made watertight with a layer of natural tar/bitumen.
  • Shape: Rectangular.
  • Believed to have been used for ritual/religious bathing — not for grain storage or farming (a common distractor option).
  • Had steps at both ends, a surrounding verandah, and small rooms — possibly for changing.

Citadel Structures

  • Included: Great Bath, Granary, Assembly Hall, and residential buildings for elites — NOT general public housing.
  • Dholavira citadel is unique for its stadium-like structure and multi-tier water reservoirs cut into rock.
4 Seals, Script, Trade & Economy

Seals High PYQ

  • Made mostly of steatite (soft stone) — commonly mistaken in options for terracotta/bronze.
  • Typically square or rectangular; carried an animal motif + a short pictographic inscription.
  • Most common animal depicted: the unicorn bull.
  • The famous "Pashupati seal" (Mohenjodaro) shows a seated three-faced figure surrounded by animals (elephant, tiger, rhinoceros, buffalo) — interpreted as a proto-Shiva figure — deer/cow are NOT part of this seal (a frequent distractor).
  • Used mainly for trade/commerce — marking ownership of goods, and possibly for administrative sealing.
  • Harappan seals have been found in Mesopotamia (Ur, Kish, Babylon), confirming direct trade contact.

Script High PYQ

  • The Indus script remains undeciphered to date — this is one of the most repeated single facts in SSC exams.
  • It is pictographic (not alphabetic), with roughly 400-plus signs; direction of writing is generally right to left (boustrophedon in some cases).
  • No confirmed relation to Brahmi, Pali, or Dravidian scripts has been established — all such "correct-looking" options are actually incorrect/unproven.

Trade & Economy High PYQ

  • Economy was based on agriculture + craft production + long-distance trade (no evidence of coinage — trade was via barter).
  • Main crops: Wheat and barley; also cultivated cotton (Harappans were the earliest producers of cotton — called "Sindon" by the Greeks), sesame, peas, rice (evidence from Lothal & Rangpur).
  • Developed the world's earliest known standardized weights and measures system — weights were cubical, mostly of chert/limestone, in a binary system (1,2,4,8,16...).
  • Trade with Mesopotamia is well attested — Mesopotamian texts refer to trade with "Meluhha" (identified with the Indus region/Harappans).
  • Lapis lazuli was imported from Badakhshan (Afghanistan); other imports included copper (from Rajasthan/Oman), tin, gold, and precious stones.
  • Lothal's dockyard is the strongest evidence of maritime/overseas trade.
  • No large temple or palace structures found — suggesting economic power may have been more decentralized/mercantile than priest- or king-dominated (a debated point, but frequently tested as "no temples found").
📍 Note: No confirmed Harappan sites found in modern Bihar — the eastern-most sites were Alamgirpur (UP) — a common "which state doesn't have IVC sites" trap in SSC (correct trap answers often include Uttarakhand or Bihar).
5 Religion & Society

Religious Beliefs Medium PYQ

  • No temples have been excavated — religion appears to have been practiced at a household/personal level, unlike Egypt/Mesopotamia.
  • Mother Goddess worship was widespread — evidenced by numerous terracotta female figurines, suggesting a fertility cult.
  • The "Proto-Shiva" / Pashupati seal suggests early Shaivite-type worship (seated yogic posture, surrounded by animals).
  • Worship of the Pipal tree is attested through seal depictions — nature worship was common.
  • Belief in amulets to ward off evil is well documented.
  • Fire altars (found at Kalibangan and Lothal) suggest ritualistic fire worship in some form.
  • Phallic (linga) and yoni worship symbols have also been found, though interpretation is debated among historians.

Social Structure

  • Society appears largely urban and egalitarian compared to later Vedic society — no strong evidence of caste-like hierarchy, though differences in house sizes suggest some economic stratification.
  • Racial composition: anthropological studies suggest a mix of Proto-Australoid, Mediterranean, Alpinoid, and Mongoloid types — the Mediterranean type is believed to be the dominant group.
  • The IVC did not know Ashvamedha (horse sacrifice) — a Vedic-period ritual, wrongly attributed to Harappans in distractor options.
  • The cow was not established as sacred in the Harappan period (that association strengthens later, in the Vedic-Puranic period) — a very common "correct answer via elimination" trap.
  • Their culture is generally described as not static — it evolved over its ~2000-year span (from Early to Mature to Late Harappan phases).

Burial Practices

  • Both extended burial (complete burial) and fractional burial (post-excarnation, bones only) are attested, along with some evidence of cremation — showing regional/temporal variation.
  • Grave goods (pottery, ornaments) were often buried with the dead, indicating belief in an afterlife.
  • Harappa shows evidence of coffin burial — a distinctive, exam-worthy detail.

Art & Craft

  • The bronze "Dancing Girl" (lost-wax/cire-perdue technique) from Mohenjodaro is the most iconic IVC sculpture.
  • The steatite "Priest-King" bust, also from Mohenjodaro.
  • Terracotta toys, figurines, and a bronze chariot (yoked to bullocks, driven by a nude male figure) found at Daimabad (Maharashtra, a Late Harappan site).
  • Pottery was typically wheel-made, red with black painted designs.
6 Theories of Decline

Major Theories High PYQ

  • Aryan Invasion Theory: Proposed by Mortimer Wheeler — argued that invading Aryans destroyed Harappan cities (based on skeletal remains found at Mohenjodaro). This theory is now largely discredited/debated by most historians but is still frequently asked as "which historian believed the decline was due to Aryan invasion?" → Wheeler.
  • Ecological/Climatic Change: Most widely accepted modern explanation — decline of monsoon, drying up of the Ghaggar-Hakra (Saraswati) river system, and increasing aridity disrupted agriculture.
  • Tectonic/Geological factors: Earthquakes and shifting river courses (e.g., the Indus changing course) may have caused flooding or abandonment of cities.
  • Floods: Repeated flooding at Mohenjodaro is evident from multiple silt layers.
  • Decline in trade: Fall of contemporary Mesopotamian trade partners may have hurt the Harappan economy.
  • Internal factors: Overuse of resources, deforestation, and administrative breakdown are also cited as contributing, gradual factors rather than a single sudden cause.
🧠 Exam tip: If a question asks for "the most accepted cause", pick ecological/climatic change; if it asks "who proposed the Aryan invasion theory", the answer is Wheeler. Many PYQs use "All of the above" as the answer for combined-cause questions (ecological change + earthquakes + Aryan attack).

Site: Mitathal — Evidence of Decline Medium PYQ

Mitathal (Haryana) is a specifically-asked site showing archaeological evidence of the decline/degeneration of the mature Harappan culture into a "post-urban" phase.
